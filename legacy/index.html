Step 1: Define Your Purpose

Why This Step Matters

Your purpose is the foundation of everything you do. It answers the fundamental questions: Where are you going? (Vision), What matters to you? (Values), and Why do you exist? (Mission). Without a clear purpose, your organization lacks direction and your team lacks motivation. This clarity helps you make better decisions, attract the right people, and stay focused when challenges arise.

Think of it this way: If you don't know where you're going, any road will take you there. Your purpose is your destination and your compass.

Step 2: Understand Your Business Proponents

Why This Step Matters

Every business has four key components that must work together: Marketing, Sales, Operations, and Administration. Understanding how each operates and what each needs to thrive is essential for strategic planning. You'll need to consider all aspects of your business and ensure each proponent has what it needs to work together to achieve your mission.

Key questions: If you were absent, could the business run itself? Could it scale without you? Your job is to strategize and decide where the business needs to go, then communicate that plan to your team.

Remember: You will need this information when you begin strategic planning. Understanding your business structure helps you identify strengths, weaknesses, and opportunities in your SWOT analysis.

Marketing

Principle: DO NOT OVER COMPLICATE. OUTSOURCE IF YOU NEED TO

Get and keep attention. Get brand in front of customers 8 times. Lead generation. Convert to sales team. Marketing includes things like branding, advertising, market research, email/social media outreach.

Sales

Principle: DO NOT COMPETE ON PRICE. COMPETE ON QUALITY!

Qualify customers before interacting. Follow up. Outreach. Build relationships. Do not try to get the sale on first call. The main purpose of Sales is to convert leads into customers. Bring money in.

Operations

Principle: TAKE TIME TO MAKE SURE OPERATIONS IS EFFICIENT

Responsible for delivering product or service to customers. Operations should be ready to react to changes in the market. Don't be afraid to outsource parts of operations.

Administration

Principle: If YOU WERE ABSENT COULD THE BUSINESS RUN ITSELF? COULD IT SCALE WITHOUT YOU?

Administration is anything in back end of business that isn't Marketing, Sales, or Operations. Examples are Accounting, I.T., H.R., Payroll, etc.

Your job is to sit at the top of Administration strategizing and deciding where the business needs to go. You must lay out a strategic plan and communicate it to the team, so they can carry it out. You must create a system that will operate and even grow without you present. Never micromanage, communicate the plan, and trust your team to carry it out.

Step 3: Create Your Business Mind Map

Why This Step Matters

A mind map helps you visualize how your business operates by mapping out each proponent and how they connect. This doesn't have to be agonizing - don't think too hard, just get it down. You'll need this information when you begin strategic planning because you must consider all aspects of your business and what each proponent needs to thrive and work together to achieve your mission.

Remember: You will refine and perfect this as time goes on, but for now, just get the basics down. This visual representation helps you see the big picture and identify connections you might have missed. This information will be crucial for your SWOT analysis in the next step.

Step 4: SWOT Analysis

Why This Step Matters

SWOT analysis helps you understand your current position before you plan your path forward. It forces you to be honest about what you're good at, what needs improvement, what opportunities exist, and what threats you face. This honest assessment is crucial because you can't formulate effective strategies without knowing where you stand. Your SWOT findings will directly inform your strategic intent and help you align your goals with reality.

Remember: These need to be identified and addressed. They need to align with your goals. This is where you formulate your strategies. Use the business structure you mapped out in the previous step to help identify your strengths and weaknesses.

Strengths

What do you do better than others? What unique capabilities and resources do you possess? What do others perceive as your strengths?

Weaknesses

What do others do better than you? What can you improve? What do others perceive as your weaknesses?

Opportunities

What opportunities are you not taking advantage of? What are others doing that you are not? Is there technology that can help you? Friends?

Threats

What trends or conditions may negatively impact you? What are others doing that may negatively impact you? Do you have solid finances? What impact do your weaknesses have on you?

Step 5: Develop Your Strategy

Why This Step Matters

Strategy is your roadmap from where you are (SWOT) to where you want to be (Vision). Without a clear strategy, you'll waste resources and effort on activities that don't move you forward. Your strategy translates your vision into actionable plans by setting goals at different time horizons, defining how you'll achieve them (Strategic Intent), identifying what you'll focus on (Drivers), and determining what capabilities you need (Enablers).

Key principle: Your strategic intent should reference your SWOT analysis - use your strengths and opportunities while addressing weaknesses and threats.

Set goals for different time horizons: 6 Month, 1 Year, 10 Year, etc. What do you want to achieve in each timeframe?

How will you get there? Refer to your SWOT analysis when formulating this.

What will you focus on? These are your major initiatives or "elephant projects" that drive progress.

What frameworks, resources, and skills will you use? What capabilities do you need to develop?

Step 6: Plan Your Execution

Why This Step Matters

A great strategy is worthless without execution. This step transforms your strategy into concrete actions and measurable outcomes. Setting targets and initiatives gives you specific things to do. Performance indicators (KPIs) tell you if you're succeeding. A strategy map helps you test and communicate your strategy to your team. Without clear execution plans, your strategy remains a wish list.

Remember: You must create a system that will operate and even grow without you present. Never micromanage - communicate the plan and trust your team to carry it out.

What will you need to do? Set specific targets and plan projects that support your goals.

How will you know you are succeeding? These metrics help you track progress and make informed decisions.

How will you test and communicate the strategy? This helps ensure everyone understands and can execute the plan.

Step 7: Review & Complete Your Plan

Why This Step Matters

Strategic planning is not a one-time event - it's an ongoing process. Setting up review frequencies and success criteria ensures your plan stays relevant and you can track progress. Regular reviews help you adjust course when needed and celebrate wins along the way. This final step makes your plan actionable and measurable.

Remember: A plan that isn't reviewed is just a document. Make time to regularly review and adjust your strategic plan.
